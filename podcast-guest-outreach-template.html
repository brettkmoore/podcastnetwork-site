How to Pitch Yourself as a Podcast Guest

Most podcast hosts love having great guests—they want to find people with interesting stories, valuable expertise, or compelling perspectives. But they receive dozens (sometimes hundreds) of guest pitch emails every month. The difference between the pitches that get ignored and the ones that get booked is personalization, clarity, and respect for the host's time.

This guide gives you actual email templates used by successful podcast guests, plus strategies for personalizing them so they work for any show. These templates have been tested and refined by hundreds of guests who are now regular podcast appearances, building authority and reaching new audiences.

The key principle: Write a pitch that shows you've actually listened to the show, understand the audience, and can offer something valuable. Make it so easy to say yes that the host would regret passing on you.

Why Personalization Is Everything

Hosts can tell immediately whether you've listened to their show. Generic pitches—the ones that could apply to any podcast—get deleted. Personalized pitches—the ones that reference specific episodes, mention the host by name, and explain why you're specifically a good fit for that audience—get seriously considered.

The Psychology of Personalization

When a host reads a pitch that mentions a recent episode they did, they think, "This person actually cares enough to listen to my show." When they see a pitch that explains how your expertise connects to the topics their audience cares about, they think, "This will make a great episode." Personalization builds trust and credibility before you ever have a conversation.

Generic pitches, by contrast, feel like spam. Even if the generic pitch is well-written, it signals to the host that you're sending the same email to 500 shows. Most hosts would rather have a less famous guest with genuine enthusiasm for their show than a famous guest who's just adding them to a bulk outreach list.

How Much Personalization Is Enough?

You don't need to write a novel. Even small personalization details make a massive difference:

  • Mention the host by name and a specific recent episode
  • Explain how your expertise connects to topics their audience cares about
  • Reference something unique about their show (their interviewing style, their audience demographic, their topic focus)
  • Keep it brief—60 seconds of reading time maximum

These small touches convert cold pitches into conversations at 3-5x the rate of generic pitches.

Template 1: The Cold Pitch (You Have No Connection)

Use this template when you have no prior relationship with the host, but you've listened to their show and identified why you're a good fit.

Cold Pitch Email
Subject: Great fit for [Show Name]: [Your expertise area]

Hi [Host Name],

I'm a [brief credential] who helps [your target audience] with [specific problem/outcome]. I've been listening to [Show Name] for [timeframe], and I particularly loved your episode on "[Specific Episode Title]" where you discussed [one key idea from that episode].

I think your audience would find value in [specific topic or insight you could bring], and I've had success [specific result or case study]. I'd love to discuss how we could create something great together.

Let me know if you'd be open to a conversation—I'm flexible with timing and happy to work around your schedule.

Best,
[Your Name]
[Your Title]
[Link to your best credential or portfolio piece]

Why This Works

This template shows you've done your homework (you know the show and can reference a specific episode), establishes your credibility quickly, explains the value for their audience (not for you), and makes it easy to say yes. The subject line is specific, the pitch is brief, and the call to action is simple.

What to Customize

  • [Host Name]: Use their first name if possible—it's more personal
  • [Show Name]: The exact podcast name
  • [Specific Episode Title]: Listen to 3-5 recent episodes and pick one that genuinely resonates with you
  • [specific topic or insight]: Make this specific to their audience, not generic
  • [specific result or case study]: Give one concrete example of impact you've created
  • [Link to credential]: Book, article, speaking video, or case study that proves what you say

The template is about 140 words—short enough to be respectful of the host's time, but specific enough to show genuine interest.

Template 2: The Warm Introduction (Someone Knows You Both)

Use this template when a mutual connection is introducing you to the host.

Warm Intro Email
Subject: [Mutual Connection] thought we should connect

Hi [Host Name],

[Mutual Connection] suggested I reach out—they thought we'd be a great fit for a conversation.

I've been listening to [Show Name] for a few months, and I really appreciate your approach to [specific thing about their show]. I work with [your audience] on [your area], and I've had some interesting results with [one specific outcome].

I think there's a great conversation here around [topic area], and I'd love to chat about whether it makes sense for your show.

[Mutual Connection] has my contact info, but I'm also here: [your email/link]

Looking forward to connecting,
[Your Name]

Why Warm Intros Are Powerful

When someone the host trusts introduces you, you skip the credibility-building phase. The host already believes you're probably worth talking to. This makes the pitch shorter and more casual—you're not selling, you're connecting.

Important Warm Intro Protocol

  • Always ask your mutual connection if they're willing to introduce you before you use their name
  • Never put words in your connection's mouth—let them make the introduction in their own voice
  • Don't claim a connection is closer than it is (e.g., "my friend [name]" when you barely know them)
  • Keep the email brief—the relationship is doing the heavy lifting, not your pitch

The best warm intro is when your mutual connection sends an email to the host saying, "I know someone I think would be great for your show, and I'm going to introduce you." Then you follow up with your pitch. It's warmer and lower-pressure than asking them to make the intro in a single email.

Template 3: The Follow-Up (First Email Didn't Get a Response)

Wait at least 2 weeks before following up. If you're going to follow up, add new value and keep it brief.

Follow-Up Email (Value Add)
Subject: Re: [Original Subject]—thought of you

Hi [Host Name],

I know you're busy, so I'll keep this short. I was just listening to your episode on [recent episode topic], and I thought of a resource that might be helpful for your audience: [specific article, tool, or insight relevant to that episode].

I still think a conversation about [topic] would create great content for your community. If you're open to it, I'm here.

Best,
[Your Name]

The Follow-Up Strategy

Most people don't follow up, which means following up itself makes you stand out. But the key is adding value in your follow-up—don't just resend your original pitch. Share something useful (an article, a tool, a thought about a recent episode) that proves you're still thinking about their show and their audience.

Follow-Up Limits

  • Wait 2+ weeks before first follow-up
  • Wait 3+ weeks before second follow-up
  • Don't send more than 2-3 follow-ups total
  • After 3 friendly attempts, let it go

Many podcast hosts are overwhelmed and may have missed your original email. A polite follow-up increases your chances significantly. But too many follow-ups can damage your reputation, so know when to stop.

Common Pitching Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake #1: Generic Pitches

Pitching the same email to 50 shows. Hosts can tell immediately. Your open rate will be terrible, and your reputation (if a host Googles you later) will be "person who sends spam pitches." Instead, spend 5 minutes listening to a show and personalizing your pitch. You'll have 3x better results.

Mistake #2: Focusing on What You Want Instead of What The Audience Needs

Bad pitch: "I'd love to be on your show to talk about my book." Good pitch: "Your audience has been asking about X, and I have a framework that might really help them." Always lead with value to the audience, not what you get out of the appearance.

Mistake #3: Pitching on Behalf of Someone Else

If you're a publicist or manager pitching on behalf of your client, you're reducing your chances dramatically. Hosts want to connect with the actual person they'll be interviewing. If you're pitching for someone else, they need to sign off, but the pitch should come from the person who will appear on the show.

Mistake #4: Vague Pitches About What You'd Discuss

Weak pitch: "I can talk about entrepreneurship." Strong pitch: "I can discuss how 3-person teams are outcompeting traditional agencies, and I have specific case studies." Be specific about what you'd talk about, not general topics. The more specific you are, the easier it is for the host to say yes.

Mistake #5: Not Including Proof

Don't just tell the host you're credible—show them. Include a link to your best work: a published article, a speaking video, a case study, or a previous podcast appearance. Make it easy for them to verify you're actually interesting.

Mistake #6: Aggressive Follow-Up

Sending follow-up emails 3 days later, then again the next week, then again after that. This feels pushy and makes you look desperate. Be patient, be respectful of their time, and know when to let it go. If they're interested, one warm follow-up after 2-3 weeks will often get a response.

Mistake #7: Making It About You, Not Them

Pitches that say "I need to grow my audience" or "This would really help my business" are less effective than pitches that lead with "Your audience is interested in X and I have insights on that." Always frame it from the listener's perspective, not yours.

The Personalization Checklist

Before you send any pitch, use this checklist to make sure you've done the work:

Before You Pitch

  • I've listened to at least 2-3 recent episodes of this show
  • I can reference a specific episode and what I liked about it
  • I understand who their audience is and what they care about
  • I can explain why my expertise is valuable to that specific audience (not generic audiences)
  • I know the host's name and can use it correctly in my email
  • I have a link to proof of my credibility (article, video, previous appearance, case study)
  • My pitch is 150-200 words maximum—respectful of their time
  • My pitch focuses on value to their audience, not benefits to me
  • I've personalized the subject line (not "Guest Pitch" or "Podcast Opportunity")
  • I've proofread and tested all links before sending

If you can check all 10 boxes, your pitch is ready to send. If you're missing items, your acceptance rate will suffer.

Frequently Asked Questions

What makes a podcast guest pitch effective?

An effective pitch is personalized to the specific show (referencing a recent episode by name), clearly states why you're a good fit for that audience, includes proof of your credibility, and makes it easy for the host to say yes. It should feel like it was written specifically for that show, not sent to 50 podcasts. The best pitches are 150-200 words, focus on value to the audience rather than benefits to the guest, and include a specific call to action.

How long should a podcast pitch email be?

Your pitch should be no more than 150-200 words. Podcast hosts are busy and receive many pitches, so brevity is critical. You should be able to convey: personalization (mentioning a specific episode), who you are (brief credential), why you're a good fit for that audience, and a call to action. If the host is interested, they'll reply asking for more details or to schedule a call.

What should I avoid when pitching to podcast hosts?

Avoid generic pitches that could apply to any show, focusing on yourself rather than the audience, vague claims about what you'd discuss, pitches on behalf of someone else without them being involved, missing contact information or credentials, aggressive follow-up (multiple emails in short succession), and sending the exact same pitch to dozens of shows without customization. The worst pitches feel like spam and get deleted immediately.

How should I follow up if I don't hear back?

Wait at least 2 weeks before sending a follow-up. When you do follow up, keep it brief and add new value—mention something from a recent episode you listened to, or share a relevant resource. Send no more than 2-3 follow-ups total. Many hosts are overwhelmed and may have missed your initial email, so a polite, brief follow-up increases your chances significantly. But know when to stop—if you've sent 3 friendly attempts with no response, let it go and move on to other shows.

Where should I find podcast contact information?

Look for contact information on the podcast's website (usually in the footer or a "contact us" page), in the show notes of episodes, on the podcast's social media profiles (Twitter, Instagram, LinkedIn), or through the host's personal website. Many podcasters list their email in anchor text at the bottom of episode show notes. LinkedIn is also an excellent resource for finding host contact information, especially their business email. As a last resort, you can look for publicists or representatives who are mentioned in show notes and reach out to them instead.
