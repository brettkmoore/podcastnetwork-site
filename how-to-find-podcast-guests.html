How to Find Great Guests for Your Podcast (Without the Headache)

Finding great podcast guests starts with knowing your audience, then searching in the places where experts already congregate: LinkedIn, podcast guest communities, matching platforms like The Podcast Network, and industry events. The best guests aren't the most famous — they're the most relevant to your listeners.

Why Guest Quality Matters More Than Guest Fame

The biggest mistake podcast hosts make? Chasing celebrity names instead of finding guests who genuinely resonate with their audience.

Here's the reality: a moderately famous guest with no connection to your listeners creates a boring episode. A perfectly-matched lesser-known expert who shares stories aligned with your audience's interests? That's gold. Your listeners will come back for more.

Quality guest selection affects everything:

  • Listener retention: Relevant guests keep your audience engaged and coming back
  • Social amplification: Great guests promote episodes to their networks, expanding your reach
  • Production efficiency: Well-prepared guests make interviews smoother and require less editing
  • Brand positioning: Your podcast becomes known for smart conversations, not celebrity chasing
  • Guest relationships: Quality interactions lead to repeat guests and referrals

The best podcasts aren't defined by who they interview—they're defined by why they interview specific people. That "why" is directly tied to audience value, not guest prestige.

7 Places to Find Podcast Guests

Stop randomly reaching out to people. Instead, go where relevant experts are already congregating. Here are seven proven sources:

1. LinkedIn Search (The Underrated Goldmine)

LinkedIn is home to thousands of experts actively looking to build their personal brands. Use LinkedIn's search filters to find people by:

  • Job title (e.g., "Content Strategist," "Product Manager," "Marketing Director")
  • Industry (be specific—"SaaS," "B2B Marketing," "AI/ML")
  • Company or company size
  • Location
  • Keywords in their headline or about section

Pro tip: Look for people with active engagement on their posts—they're already comfortable being in the spotlight. Check if they mention speaking experience or content creation in their profiles.

2. Podcast Guest Communities (Skool, Facebook Groups)

Communities like Skool and private Facebook groups are specifically designed for people seeking podcast appearances. These folks are pre-qualified: they want to be on podcasts. You just need to make sure they're relevant to your show.

  • Skool Communities: Search for podcast guest communities in your niche. Members actively pitch themselves.
  • Facebook Groups: Groups like "Podcast Guests for Female Entrepreneurs" or industry-specific forums are treasure troves.
  • Reddit: Subreddits like r/podcasts often have people seeking guest opportunities.

3. Matching Platforms Like The Podcast Network

Dedicated podcast guest booking platforms streamline the process by matching you with pre-vetted guests. Here's why this works:

  • Guests are already vetted for audio quality, reliability, and speaking ability
  • Matching algorithms suggest guests aligned with your show's niche and audience
  • Automated scheduling and communication save hours of back-and-forth
  • You get access to detailed guest bios and past speaking experience
  • Quality control is handled upfront, so you're not screening dozens of unqualified candidates

While not free, these platforms often pay for themselves through time savings alone.

4. Industry Conferences and Events

Attend or sponsor industry conferences relevant to your podcast niche. Speakers at these events are:

  • Already polished presenters with speaking experience
  • Deeply knowledgeable about their topics
  • Often looking for additional visibility opportunities
  • Easier to approach in person (less cold than email)

Even if you can't attend in person, many conferences publish speaker lists online. A personal outreach mentioning the conference creates an immediate connection.

5. Author and Book Launches

Authors launching books are in "visibility mode." They're actively seeking interview opportunities and podcast appearances. Resources:

  • Publishers' PR teams: Many publishers actively pitch authors to media outlets
  • Amazon bestseller lists: New releases in your industry are often from authors seeking exposure
  • Book deal announcements: Publishers announce new books on social media
  • Goodreads releases: Track upcoming releases in your niche

Timing matters: reach out 4-6 weeks before the book launch for the best response rates.

6. Complementary Podcasts' Audiences

Guest on other podcasts in your niche, and those podcasts' audiences often have guest recommendations. Additionally:

  • Listen to shows similar to yours and note recurring guests—they're clearly good speakers
  • Check podcast show notes and guest bios
  • Follow podcasters in your niche on social media and note who they interview
  • Join podcast networks or communities where hosts discuss guests

7. Your Own Audience

Don't overlook the easiest source: your listeners. Many of your best audience members have insights worth sharing. Methods:

  • Ask in email newsletters: "Do you have expertise to share? We'd love to feature you."
  • Create a simple form for audience members to pitch themselves as guests
  • Monitor comments and DMs for engaged listeners with interesting perspectives
  • Host listener Q&As where audience members become guests

This builds community, gives your most loyal listeners recognition, and often leads to passionate promotion from their networks.

How to Evaluate a Potential Guest

Not every expert is a good podcast guest. Before extending an invitation, assess these four key factors:

1. Relevance to Your Audience

Ask yourself: Will this guest's expertise, story, or perspective genuinely benefit my listeners? Relevance includes:

  • Topic alignment with your podcast's focus
  • Expertise level (are they actually knowledgeable, or just self-proclaimed?)
  • Audience stage (does the guest appeal to your listeners' current needs?)
  • Unique angle (what will this guest add that you haven't covered before?)

2. Speaking Ability

A knowledgeable guest with poor communication skills creates a painful episode. Research their speaking experience:

  • Listen to past podcast appearances (check their LinkedIn or website)
  • Watch conference talks or webinar recordings on YouTube
  • Check for "umms," filler words, and speaking clarity
  • Assess whether they tell stories or just list facts
  • Review how they handle questions and push-back

If you can't find recorded content, conduct a brief test call before committing to recording.

3. Promotional Reach

A great episode matters less if the guest doesn't help promote it. Evaluate:

  • Social media following (do they have an engaged audience?)
  • Email list size (will they promote to subscribers?)
  • Industry influence (does their endorsement carry weight?)
  • Willingness to promote (have they committed to sharing the episode?)

Note: This doesn't mean only booking guests with large followings. But their willingness to promote to whatever audience they have matters.

4. Story Angle

The best episodes tell compelling stories. Before booking, confirm:

  • Does the guest have a clear, interesting story to share?
  • Is there a personal narrative (not just credentials)?
  • What's the transformation or key insight they offer?
  • Can you summarize the episode's value in one sentence?

The Perfect Guest Outreach Message

Your outreach email determines whether a potential guest even considers your invitation. Here's a template that works:

Subject: Your expertise would resonate with [Podcast Name] listeners Hi [Guest First Name], I've followed your work in [specific area] for a while now, and I was impressed by [specific thing—article, talk, LinkedIn post, etc.]. I host a podcast called [Podcast Name], and we focus on [one sentence about your show]. Your perspective on [specific topic] would resonate incredibly well with our listeners. I'd love to have you on for a casual 45-minute conversation about [general topic]. Here's what makes [Podcast Name] different: • [One specific differentiator] • [Another differentiator—e.g., "we reach 10k monthly listeners in B2B marketing"] • [Third differentiator—e.g., "guests average 2x social shares compared to industry podcasts"] The episode would be published on [date range], and I'll handle all promotion through our email list (X subscribers) and social channels. No prep burden—we'll do a quick 15-minute call beforehand to align on topics. Would you be interested? I'm flexible on timing and happy to work around your schedule. Best, [Your name] [Podcast name] [Website or link to show]

Personalization Tips

  • Be specific: Reference actual work they've done, not generic praise
  • Lead with audience value: Show how appearing helps them reach new people
  • Highlight differentiators: Why your show is worth their time
  • Remove friction: Emphasize ease of participation and flexible scheduling
  • Include proof: Metrics, testimonials, or past guest results
  • Keep it short: Under 200 words. Busy people skim.

Follow-Up Strategy

  • Send initial outreach on Tuesday-Thursday (highest open rates)
  • Follow up after 5-7 days if no response
  • Try a different angle in the second follow-up (different value prop)
  • After second follow-up, move on—respect their time

How to Build a Guest Pipeline

Consistent great episodes require a consistent flow of great guests. Here's how to build a system:

Batch Your Guest Sessions

Record multiple episodes in one day (called "batching"). This approach:

  • Reduces scheduling complexity (guests only need one time slot)
  • Allows you to focus on interview quality
  • Creates content buffer (record 4 episodes in one day = one month's content)
  • Enables bulk post-production work

Best practice: Set one "recording day" per month (first Monday, for example). Invite 4-5 guests. Even if one cancels, you're covered.

Create a Content Calendar with Guest Slots

Build your editorial calendar with guest appearances planned 6-8 weeks in advance. This allows:

  • Time for guest pitching and confirmation
  • Aligned themes and topics (e.g., "Q2 focuses on growth marketing")
  • Mixed solo and guest episodes for variety
  • Strategic guest selection (don't cluster similar guests)

Always Have a Backup List

Guests cancel. It happens. Maintain a list of "ready-to-record" guests:

  • Contacts who've expressed willingness to appear anytime
  • Past guests open to returning
  • Internal team members or co-founders for filler episodes

Create Recurring Guest Slots

Some of your best guests can become recurring segments:

  • "Industry Expert Q&A" with a rotating guest each month
  • Weekly "Quick Wins" with different entrepreneurs
  • Monthly roundtables with complementary guests

Recurring slots eliminate the need to find new guests for every episode and create listener anticipation.

Common Guest-Finding Mistakes (And How to Avoid Them)

Mistake 1: Chasing Only Big Names

The biggest names often have the least engagement because they're disconnected from niche communities.

Solution: Focus on the "micro-famous"—people with 10k-100k engaged followers in your specific niche. They're more responsive, more likely to promote, and often more interesting conversationalists.

Mistake 2: Not Vetting Before Booking

Skipping the evaluation phase can lead to uncomfortable episodes, no-shows, or guests who don't align with your brand.

Solution: Always require a 15-20 minute prep call before recording. Test their audio quality, confirm their expertise, and assess how well they fit your show.

Mistake 3: Booking Without a Promotion Plan

A great guest means nothing if nobody hears the episode.

Solution: Before booking, confirm how both you and the guest will promote the episode. Clarify expectations around social shares, email mentions, and timing.

Mistake 4: Inconsistent Outreach

Reaching out randomly means inconsistent bookings and constantly scrambling.

Solution: Set a monthly outreach cadence. Target 5-10 guests per month, even if you only need 2-3. This creates a healthy pipeline.

Mistake 5: Over-Relying on One Source

If you only recruit guests from LinkedIn, you'll miss the diverse talent in other communities.

Solution: Diversify. Use 3-4 sources consistently. Each has different guest profiles and communities.

Mistake 6: Poor Follow-Up

Many potential guests don't respond to one email. That doesn't mean they're not interested—they might be busy.

Solution: Always follow up. One follow-up email increases response rates by 50%+. Two follow-ups? Even better. But know when to stop (after two, let it go).

Mistake 7: Ignoring Guest Feedback

If guests repeatedly mention concerns or friction, that's data worth listening to.

Solution: After each episode, ask guests for feedback. What was smooth? What was confusing? Use this to improve the experience for future guests.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do podcast hosts find guests?

Podcast hosts find guests through multiple channels: LinkedIn searches, podcast guest communities on platforms like Skool and Facebook, dedicated matching services like The Podcast Network, industry conferences and events, author book launches, complementary podcasts' audiences, and their own listener base. The most effective approach combines multiple sources and focuses on relevance to your audience rather than guest fame.

Where can I find podcast guests for free?

You can find podcast guests for free on LinkedIn (using advanced search filters), Facebook groups dedicated to podcast guests, Skool communities, Twitter/X, and your existing audience. Industry conferences and virtual events also provide free networking opportunities. Additionally, reaching out to authors, thought leaders, and complementary podcasters directly often costs nothing but time.

How many guests should I book per month?

The ideal number of guests per month depends on your publishing schedule. If you publish weekly, aim for 3-4 guests per month. If you publish twice weekly, target 6-8. Most successful podcasters recommend batching guest sessions (recording 3-4 episodes in one day) and maintaining a 6-8 week content calendar buffer to prevent last-minute scrambling.

Should I pay for a podcast guest booking service?

It depends on your goals and resources. Free methods work well for niche podcasts with specific audiences. Paid services like The Podcast Network offer valuable benefits: pre-vetted guests, quality control, better matching algorithms, and time savings. If you value efficiency and want consistent guest quality without the research burden, a paid service often pays for itself through reduced screening time and better episode quality.

How do I vet potential podcast guests?

Vet potential guests by assessing: relevance to your audience (do they add value?), speaking ability (listen to past podcast appearances or videos), promotional reach (will they help promote the episode?), and story angle (do they have a compelling narrative?). Always do a prep call before recording to test audio quality, confirm availability, and align on discussion topics.
